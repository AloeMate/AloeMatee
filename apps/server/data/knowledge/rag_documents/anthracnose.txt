DISEASE: Anthracnose
SCIENTIFIC NAME: Colletotrichum gloeosporioides, Colletotrichum aloeae
CATEGORY: Fungal Disease
AFFECTS: Leaves and occasionally stems of Aloe vera

SYMPTOMS:
- Small, sunken, water-soaked spots that enlarge into oval or irregular lesions
- Lesion centres turn tan, grey, or light brown with dark brown or black borders
- In humid conditions, salmon-pink or orange spore masses appear in lesion centres
- Lesions may merge to form large dead areas covering much of the leaf
- Tip dieback - leaf tips die back progressively toward the base
- Severely infected leaves turn completely brown and die
- Disease progresses faster in warm, wet weather

CAUSES:
- Colletotrichum fungi thrive in warm (22-30°C), humid conditions
- Overhead irrigation or rain splashing spores between plants
- Poor air circulation and overcrowding
- Wounds on leaves (from handling, insects, or physical damage) provide entry points
- Introduction of infected cuttings or plants
- Spores can survive in soil and plant debris for long periods

SCIENTIFIC TREATMENT:
Step 1 - Quarantine the infected plant: Immediately move away from all other plants. Anthracnose spreads rapidly by water splash.
Step 2 - Remove and destroy all infected tissue: Cut off all leaves showing lesions using sterilized scissors. Remove even leaves with small spots - early removal prevents spread. Seal in plastic bag, dispose with household waste.
Step 3 - Apply systemic fungicide: Spray with thiophanate-methyl (0.1% solution) or azoxystrobin-based fungicide. These systemic fungicides are absorbed into plant tissue and kill the fungus from inside. Follow product label exactly for dilution and safety precautions.
Step 4 - Follow with contact fungicide: 3 days after systemic application, spray copper oxychloride (0.25% solution) as a contact protective barrier on all remaining healthy leaf surfaces.
Step 5 - Repeat treatment cycle: Apply systemic fungicide every 10-14 days for 3 applications. Monitor for new lesions between applications.
Step 6 - Sanitation: Remove all fallen plant debris from around the pot. Do not allow dead leaves to accumulate - they harbour fungal spores.

AYURVEDIC / NATURAL TREATMENT:
Step 1 - Remove infected tissue as described above.
Step 2 - Neem oil + baking soda spray: Mix 2 tablespoons neem oil, 1 tablespoon baking soda, 1 teaspoon castile soap in 1 litre warm water. Baking soda changes surface pH making it hostile to fungal growth. Apply every 5-7 days.
Step 3 - Turmeric + neem paste: Mix turmeric powder with liquid neem oil to form a paste. Apply directly onto lesion edges to prevent spread. Curcumin in turmeric inhibits Colletotrichum spore germination.
Step 4 - Compost tea drench: Brew vermicompost tea (1 cup compost in 4 litres water, aerate for 24 hours). Apply to soil. Beneficial microorganisms suppresses Colletotrichum in soil.
Step 5 - Wood ash dusting: Lightly dust wood ash on cut surfaces after removing infected leaves. Wood ash contains potassium and calcium, raises surface pH, and inhibits fungal growth.

DOSAGE AND FREQUENCY:
- Systemic fungicide: Every 10-14 days, 3 applications minimum
- Neem oil spray: Every 5-7 days until symptoms clear, then every 2 weeks as prevention
- Do not mix different chemical fungicides together

SAFETY WARNINGS:
- Thiophanate-methyl and azoxystrobin are potential endocrine disruptors - always wear gloves, mask, and eye protection
- Do not apply systemic fungicides within 4 weeks if harvesting any part of the plant for medicinal use
- Copper compounds are toxic to aquatic life - do not wash spray equipment near drains or water bodies
- Keep all fungicide-treated plants away from children and pets until spray is completely dry

PREVENTION:
- Always sterilize cutting tools before propagating aloe vera
- Inspect all new plants carefully before introducing to your collection
- Avoid wetting leaves when watering - use drip irrigation or water at soil level only
- Remove and dispose of all fallen/dead leaves promptly
- Ensure good air circulation - maintain 30cm spacing between plants minimum

WHEN TO CONSULT AN EXPERT:
- Disease spreads despite 3 rounds of fungicide treatment
- More than 60% of leaves are affected
- Plant shows signs of stem collapse in addition to leaf lesions
- Lesions appear on very young new growth or the plant centre
- You cannot identify a treatment product available in your local area
