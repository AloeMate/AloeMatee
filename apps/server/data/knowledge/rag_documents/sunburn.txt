DISEASE: Sunburn
SCIENTIFIC NAME: Non-infectious abiotic disorder (photo-oxidative stress)
CATEGORY: Environmental / Abiotic Injury
AFFECTS: Leaves and occasionally growing tip of Aloe vera

SYMPTOMS:
- White, pale yellow, or bleached patches on areas of leaf directly exposed to intense light
- Patches typically appear on the upper (adaxial) surface of leaves facing the sun
- Affected tissue gradually turns tan, brown, or silvery-white
- Severe sunburn: large sections of leaves turn completely brown, papery, and dry
- Tissue damage is irreversible - injured cells do not recover
- Plant does not deteriorate further once moved to appropriate light conditions
- No spreading to adjacent leaves (not infectious)
- Often occurs shortly after moving a plant from shade to direct sun (acute sunburn)
- Can also develop gradually over weeks on plants kept in too-bright a location (chronic burn)
- Distinguished from fungal diseases by absence of spots with halos, spore masses, or water-soaked margins

CAUSES:
- Excessive direct sunlight, especially afternoon sun (UV + infrared combined)
- Rapid transition from a shaded to a bright outdoor location without acclimatisation
- Plants previously grown indoors placed directly in full outdoor sun
- Reflected light from walls, windows, or metal surfaces concentrating light intensity
- Bright light combined with water stress (dehydrated plants are more susceptible)
- Temperature extremes amplify light damage (hot afternoon sun above 38°C)

SCIENTIFIC TREATMENT:
Step 1 - Immediately move to filtered light: Relocate plant to a position receiving bright indirect light or dappled shade (4-6 hours of gentle morning sun, protected from afternoon sun). Do not abruptly move to full shade as this creates a different stress.
Step 2 - Do not remove damaged leaves immediately: Sunburned leaves act as a physical barrier protecting inner leaves while the plant adjusts. Remove only leaves that are completely desiccated and dead, or those that are collapsing.
Step 3 - Check hydration: Sunburn worsens in dehydrated plants. Verify soil moisture - if the top 3 cm of soil is dry, water thoroughly. Allow water to drain completely. Proper hydration supports cellular repair in remaining healthy tissue.
Step 4 - Apply aloe-safe silica spray: Potassium silicate or orthosilicic acid foliar spray (0.1% solution) hardens epidermal cells and improves heat and UV resistance. Apply weekly during recovery.
Step 5 - Gradual light acclimatisation: After 3-4 weeks in filtered light, very slowly increase light exposure by 1 hour per week. This allows the plant to upregulate its UV-protective flavonoid and anthocyanin pigments.
Step 6 - Remove dead tissue after recovery: Once the plant shows active new growth, trim away the brown desiccated sections using sterilized scissors. Seal cut surfaces with powdered cinnamon or activated charcoal to prevent opportunistic fungal infection.

AYURVEDIC / NATURAL TREATMENT:
Step 1 - Move to appropriate light as described above.
Step 2 - Aloe vera self-healing gel: Ironically, aloe vera's own gel has documented wound-healing properties. If only a small leaf tip is burned, the plant will naturally seal the damaged area. Do not apply external gel preparations unnecessarily.
Step 3 - Neem oil protective coating: After recovery begins, apply dilute neem oil spray (1 tablespoon per litre water) to coat leaves. Neem forms a thin protective layer reducing UV penetration and retaining moisture in the leaf cuticle.
Step 4 - Mulch with coarse material: Place light-coloured coarse gravel or pumice stone on the soil surface. This reflects some light away from the pot base, reduces soil temperature, and keeps root zone cooler.
Step 5 - Compost tea boost: Apply compost tea to soil (not leaves) to support root health. Well-nourished roots provide better water uptake, and hydrated plants recover faster from sun stress. Use fortnightly during recovery.

DOSAGE AND FREQUENCY:
- Silica spray: Once per week during recovery, then fortnightly as ongoing prevention
- Compost tea: Fortnightly soil drench (not foliar application)
- Neem leaf coating: Every 2 weeks as maintenance

SAFETY WARNINGS:
- Sunburned aloe vera gel may have slightly altered chemical composition - consult a pharmacist before using sunburned portions medicinally
- Do not apply any oils, creams, or sprays to plants in direct hot sun - products heat up and can further damage leaves
- When moving plants outdoors in summer, always start with morning sun (before 10 AM) only - never afternoon sun

PREVENTION:
- Acclimatise all indoor plants to outdoor conditions over 2-3 weeks by gradually increasing sun exposure
- In hot climates (above 35°C average summer), use 30-40% shade cloth over aloe during peak summer months
- Position plants to receive morning sun (east-facing) and afternoon shade
- Ensure plants are well-watered before placing in sunny locations
- Avoid placing plants near heat-reflecting surfaces (south-facing whitewashed walls, metal fences, glass)
- Regularly inspect plants during heatwaves and move to shade if signs of stress appear

WHEN TO CONSULT AN EXPERT:
- Sunburn damage is accompanied by signs of root rot (mushy base, foul smell) suggesting compounding of stressors
- New leaves emerging after moving plant to filtered light are also showing bleaching (indicates light levels still too high or a different underlying problem)
- Brown patches show spore masses, halos, or water-soaked margins suggesting fungal disease rather than sunburn
- Plant fails to produce any new growth after 8-10 weeks in appropriate conditions
